Question 1: Boston Crime Districts

The Haunted Districts of Boston

A chilling investigation into crime patterns across Boston's districts from 2015-2022

Key Insights

  • Overall Crime Trends: Most districts show a general decline in crime from 2015 to 2022, with the most dramatic reduction occurring during the COVID-19 pandemic (April-May 2020), where crime dropped by 30-50% across all districts.
  • District-Specific Patterns: B2 (Roxbury/Mission Hill) consistently shows the highest crime counts (1000-1500 incidents/month), while A15 (Charlestown) has the lowest crime rates (100-200 incidents/month). External district appears only from late 2019 with minimal activity.
  • Seasonal Variations: Summer months (June-August) typically show 10-15% higher crime rates across most districts, likely due to increased outdoor activity and longer daylight hours.
  • COVID-19 Impact: The pandemic created a sustained reduction in crime that hasn't fully returned to pre-2020 levels, suggesting lasting changes in urban activity patterns.

Alternative Visualization Ideas

  • Stacked Area Chart: Shows total city crime while revealing district composition over time
  • Interactive Heatmap: District vs. Month with color intensity representing crime density
  • Animated Choropleth Map: Geographic visualization showing crime hotspots moving over time
  • Dashboard with Drill-Down: Overview with ability to explore specific districts and time periods interactively
  • Small Multiples Bar Chart: Monthly comparisons with better categorical perception

Visualization Drawbacks

  • Comparison Difficulty: Independent Y-axes make cross-district comparisons challenging - can't easily compare absolute crime counts between districts
  • Information Overload: 13 small charts can overwhelm viewers and make it difficult to identify overall patterns
  • Lack of Geographic Context: No spatial understanding of where districts are located or their relationships to each other
  • Limited Interactivity: Static view doesn't allow focusing on specific patterns or filtering by time periods
  • Missing Context: No population normalization means high-population districts may appear more dangerous than they actually are
Question 2: US Mobility During Pandemic

When America Stopped Moving

Tracking the ghostly emptiness of streets, parks, and workplaces during COVID-19

Key Insights

  • Immediate Pandemic Response: March 13, 2020 (National Emergency) triggered a 40-50% drop in workplace and retail mobility within two weeks
  • Inverse Relationship: As workplace mobility decreased by 40%, residential mobility increased by 15-20%, reflecting the massive work-from-home shift
  • Parks Anomaly: Parks showed increased usage during good weather despite lockdowns, reaching +80% during summer 2020, indicating outdoor recreation became a critical pandemic outlet
  • Recovery Patterns Varied: Grocery/Pharmacy recovered quickest (essential services), Parks exceeded baseline during summers, while Transit/Workplace showed slowest recovery and remained below baseline even in 2022
  • Holiday Signatures: Consistent drops during major holidays (Thanksgiving -70%, Christmas -80%) reveal strong seasonal patterns across all years

Alternative Visualization Ideas

  • Calendar Heatmap: Daily mobility values shown as colored tiles for easy pattern recognition
  • Radar Chart Animation: Shows changing mobility profile over time with all categories visible simultaneously
  • Small Multiples with Shared Y-axis: Better comparison across categories while maintaining individual trends
  • Slope Chart: Comparing key time points (pre-pandemic, peak lockdown, current) for dramatic contrast
  • Diverging Bar Chart: Show positive/negative changes from baseline more clearly
Question 3: US Vaccination Map

The Vaccination Spell Spreads

Mapping the life-saving vaccination rollout across America

Geographic & Policy Insights

  • Geographic Disparities: Clear regional patterns with Northeast and West Coast showing higher vaccination rates (70-80%) compared to Southern and Mountain states (50-60%)
  • Political Correlation: States with higher vaccination rates generally align with specific political demographics, suggesting policy and cultural factors heavily influence uptake
  • Population Density Factor: Urban states tend to have higher vaccination rates than rural states, possibly due to better healthcare access and different risk perceptions

Color Scheme Analysis: Binned vs Continuous

  • ‚úì Binned is Better Because: Vaccination rates have meaningful policy thresholds (50%, 70% for herd immunity), makes it easier to identify states in critical ranges, better for colorblind accessibility, and clearer for policy makers to identify intervention targets
  • Continuous Would Be Better If: Showing subtle gradients between similar states, tracking small changes over time, or emphasizing exact percentages rather than categories
  • Verdict: The binned color scheme is appropriate for this dataset because policy thresholds matter more than precise percentages

Color Scheme Recommendations

  • Binned (Current): Better for identifying discrete categories and state-by-state comparison
  • Continuous Gradient: Better for showing smooth variations and precise values
  • Diverging Scale: Could highlight above/below average vaccination rates
  • Sequential with More Bins: Compromise between categorical clarity and gradient detail
Question 4: Global COVID-19 Race

The Pandemic's Deadly Race

Watch as COVID-19 cases surge across the globe in this haunting animation

Key Insights

  • Shifting Epicenters: The pandemic's center moved from China (Jan-Feb 2020) to Europe (March 2020) to the Americas (April 2020 onward), showing clear global transmission patterns
  • US Dominance: The US maintained the #1 position from April 2020 through 2022, representing about 20% of global cases despite having only 4% of world population
  • India's Delta Surge: India's dramatic climb during the Delta wave (April-May 2021) shows how new variants can rapidly change global rankings
  • Exponential Growth Patterns: Countries showed similar S-curve growth patterns but offset by weeks/months, indicating predictable spread dynamics
  • Persistence of Rankings: After initial volatility, top 5 countries (US, India, Brazil, France, Germany) remained relatively stable, suggesting systemic factors beyond initial response
  • Testing Correlation: Countries with higher testing capacity showed higher case counts, while China's early exit from top 10 despite large population suggests reporting inconsistencies

Alternative Visualization Ideas

  • Choropleth Map Animation: Show geographic spread with time slider
  • Line Chart with Highlighting: Track all countries with interactive selection
  • Bump Chart: Show ranking changes over time more clearly than bar race
  • Small Multiples Line Charts: Compare top 10 countries side-by-side
  • Slope Chart: Compare beginning vs end rankings dramatically

Bar Chart Race Drawbacks

  • Analysis vs Entertainment: Great for engagement and storytelling but poor for detailed quantitative analysis
  • Lost Data: Countries dropping out of top 10 disappear from view entirely, hiding important pandemic stories
  • Speed Issues: Animation speed makes it hard to read exact values or pause at critical moments
  • No Cumulative Context: Difficult to see overall pandemic progression or total global burden
  • Missing Per-Capita: Absolute counts don't account for population differences - smaller countries with high per-capita rates are invisible
  • Wave Patterns Hidden: Multiple surges visible as acceleration periods but hard to analyze systematically
